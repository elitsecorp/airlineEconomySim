How the simulation is calculated

This page explains the formulas behind the dashboard, the reasoning behind the cut logic, and why some values are annual totals while others are per-flight estimates.

Back to dashboard

1) Revenue model

Route demand changes with fuel price through price pass-through and elasticity.

Ticket price: ticket_price = base_ticket_price × fuel_pass_through(fuel_price)

Fuel pass-through: fuel_pass_through = 1.0 + 0.10 × (fuel_price - 1.0)

Demand: demand = base_demand × (price / base_price) ^ elasticity

Passenger revenue: passengers × ticket_price

Ancillary revenue: a small add-on share of passenger revenue, used to approximate bags, seat choice, and other extras.

Reasoning: business demand is less sensitive to price than leisure demand, so the model uses a mild elasticity for business travelers and a stronger one for leisure travelers.

2) Cost model

Each flight has fuel, crew, maintenance, and airport costs.

Fuel cost: distance × fuel_burn × fuel_price

Crew cost: fixed amount per flight, higher for widebodies

Maintenance cost: distance × maintenance_per_km

Airport fees: fixed amount per flight

Total cost: fuel + crew + maintenance + airport fees

Reasoning: longer flights and larger aircraft burn more fuel and create more operating cost pressure, especially when fuel prices rise.

3) Route viability

Routes are marked watch or cut depending on how close they are to break-even.

Per-route profit: profit = revenue - cost

Break-even fuel threshold: breakeven_fuel = 1 + (baseline_profit / baseline_fuel_cost) - (distance / 5000)

Watch threshold: watch_fuel = 0.90 × breakeven_fuel

Cut rule: if current fuel price is at or above the break-even threshold, the route is suspended.

Reasoning: long-haul routes are more exposed to fuel shocks, so the distance penalty pushes them into risk sooner than short domestic routes.

4) Pilot workload

The gauge shows how cut routes reduce flying hours per pilot.

Workload share: current_flight_share × (0.6 + 0.4 × passenger_share)

Expected hours per pilot: workload_share × 100

Hours lost per pilot: 100 - expected_hours_per_pilot

Total required hours: pilot_count × 100 × workload_share

Reasoning: pilot flying time falls when routes are cut, and passenger reductions pull the utilization down even further.

5) Annual totals vs per-flight figures

The dashboard shows both because they answer different questions.

Income statement: annual totals are scaled to Ethiopian Airlines' published financial statement figures.

Route panel: per-flight values are flight-level estimates for a single route leg.

Revenue structure: the pie chart shows the annual mix of business lines.

Cost structure: the pie chart shows annual cost buckets, reweighted as fuel prices move.

Reasoning: one chart needs company-level scale, while the route detail panel needs leg-level economics. The simulation keeps both views separate to avoid mixing units.

6) Important caveat

This is a simulation, not an audited forecasting model.

Public financial reports do not disclose every route's real per-flight economics, so the route-level values are modeled from published company totals, route characteristics, and fuel sensitivity. The formulas are transparent so you can see exactly how the dashboard reacts.
